Physics & Math

1. Coordinate System & Geometry

The balloon is placed at the origin. The camera views it from distance d_cam at a look-down angle θ below horizontal. The camera-to-balloon direction vector is d_view = [cos(θ), 0, -sin(θ)]. Camera altitude above ground: cam_alt = balloon_alt + d_cam · sin(θ).

2. Surface Normal & Reflection

For each pixel (u, v) on the balloon disk (u² + v² < 1), the depth component is w = √(1 - u² - v²). The outward surface normal is N = -d_view·w + cam_right·u + cam_up·v (normalized). The reflected ray R = I - 2(I·N)N determines whether the pixel reflects sky (R_z > 0) or ground (R_z < 0).

3. Key Physical Insights

  • Center = retroreflector: The center of the sphere reflects the viewing ray back toward its source. At 8° look-down, the center reflects sky at 8° elevation — cold, not hot.
  • Sky/ground boundary near equator: At shallow look-down angles, the boundary between sky-reflecting and ground-reflecting regions sits near the visual equator, giving the characteristic half-dark / half-bright appearance.
  • Hottest point is NOT at the bottom: The hottest ground reflection is where the reflected ray points most steeply downward (shortest slant path). This is below center but well above the bottom limb. At the very bottom, the reflected ray approaches horizontal, giving enormous ground paths and high atmospheric attenuation.
  • Atmospheric shortcut effect: The balloon's ground-reflecting region can appear warmer than the background ground because the total reflected path (camera→balloon→ground) can be shorter than the direct camera→background path, resulting in less attenuation of the hot ground signal.
  • Atmosphere as equalizer: At long ranges or high extinction, everything converges to atmospheric temperature. Ground, sky reflections, and background all merge toward Tatm, destroying contrast.
  • White-hot ground exposure: When the camera AGC is set for ground contrast, the sky-reflecting upper half saturates black (much colder), while the ground-reflecting lower half appears as a bright crescent.

4. Sky Temperature Model

T_sky(elev) = T_zenith + (T_horizon - T_zenith) · (1 - sin(elev))^0.6, where T_horizon = T_zenith + 35K. The horizon is warmest; the zenith is coldest.

5. Atmospheric Transmission

Beer-Lambert: τ(d) = exp(-β · d). Path radiance: L_received = L_source · τ(d) + L_atm · (1 - τ(d)). The atmosphere both attenuates the source and adds its own emission.

6. Planck Radiance & Band Integration

Spectral radiance B(T, λ) = (2hc²/λ&sup5;) / (exp(hc/λkT) - 1). Band-integrated over MWIR (3.5–4.15 μm and 4.4–4.9 μm), avoiding the CO&sub2; absorption at 4.2–4.4 μm.

7. Mylar Reflection

L_reflected = R_mylar · L_source + ε_mylar · L(T_balloon), where ε = 1 - R and T_balloon ≈ T_atm - 10K. High reflectivity mylar (R ≈ 0.97) means the balloon is almost a perfect mirror in MWIR.
